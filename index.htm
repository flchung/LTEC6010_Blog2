Information Processing Theory

According to Lawless (2019), Information Processing Theory is a cognitive theory that focuses on how information is encoded into our memory. First, the sensory input is any kind of stimulation that we can see, hear, smell, feel, or taste. So that we can perceive something through our sensory memory in a given moment. These inputs will become a form of information that the brain can process. However, sensory memories are unconscious and last for up to three seconds, so we usually forget them quickly. After we pay attention to those sensory memories, they will become short-term memory which can last for about 20 seconds. But we need to repeat memorizing to hold the information until they become long-term memory, or we will lose the information. Long-term memory includes various types of information and has unlimited space. The important factors are proper encoding and retrieval processes to organize the information well.

Reinforcement Theory

Reinforcement theory, "is the process of shaping behavior by controlling the consequences of behavior" (Barnett, n.d.). By using both rewards and punishments to reinforce desired behavior or extinguish unwanted behavior. According to

Skinner who defined the model, there is an outline about four methods of conditioning:

1. Positive reinforcement strengthens a behavior by providing a consequence an individual finds rewarding (McLeod, 2018).

2. Negative reinforcement strengthens behavior because it stops or removes an unpleasant experience (McLeod, 2018).

3. Positive punishment strengthens a behavior by providing a consequence an individual finds rewarding (McLeod, 2018).

4. Negative punishment (also called extinction) strengthens behavior because it stops or removes an unpleasant experience (McLeod, 2018).

Schedules of Reinforcement

Both of time and frequency of giving reinforcement play a significant role in learning process of operant conditioning (Van Wagner, 2010b). There are two types of reinforcement schedules.

1. Continuous reinforcement is when a desired behavior is reinforced each and every time it is displayed (Redmond, 2016).

2. Intermittent reinforcement is when a desired behavior is reinforced only occasionally when it is displayed (Redmond, 2016). According to “Reinforcement Theory of Motivation” (2018), there are many variations of intermittent reinforcement, including:

(1) Fixed-interval reinforcement: happens when reinforcement is given at set times.

(2) Variable interval reinforcement: happens when reinforcement is given regularly but not at set times.

(3) Fixed ratio reinforcement: happens when a fixed number of actions have been performed.

(4) Variable ratio reinforcement: happens when a variable number of actions have been performed.

Scaffolding Theory

According to Sarikas (2018), “scaffolding is a teaching method that helps students learn more by working with a teacher or a more advanced student to achieve their learning goals.” (para. 3) It is a process in which teachers model or demonstrate how to solve a problem, and then step back, offering support as needed (Firestone, n.d.). The theory is that instructors afford subsequent levels of temporary support that help students reach higher levels of comprehension and skill acquisition that they are less likely to achieve without assistance (The Glossary of Education Reform, 2015). Students learn more when collaborating with others who have a wider range of skills and knowledge than the student currently does.

 

The Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD)

The zone of proximal development (ZPD) has been defined as:

"the distance between the actual developmental level as determined by independent problem solving and the level of potential development as determined through problem-solving under adult guidance, or in collaboration with more capable peers" (Vygotsky, 1978, p. 86).
